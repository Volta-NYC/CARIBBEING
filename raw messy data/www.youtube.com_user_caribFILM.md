Razorshop's Laventille Rhythm Section Library DJ Demo TEST.
.

Artistic Statement
.
.
The possibilities of a Jouvay morning have always been the highlight of my years living in Brooklyn. It gave me a space to show my American friends what home means to me, the depth of my people’s practices of resistance. It contained some of my bravest strides to defy power as a Caribbean person, to dance low in the middle of the road as an immigrant, taking up space in a land where I owned nothing but my passport and fetishied accent. It meant asserting my freedom and power as a woman, able to refuse or accept a wine at will.

Before the sun starts squinting at Flatbush Avenue between the garden and zoo, rewilded spaces in a city of structures, it also meant walking in a big group, with a plan to look out for each other at all times, dodging of instances of violence, the stabbings and shootings that the Mayor laments about every year. When the sun slowly mounts the chilly back of September, my sense of safety is restored, my sense of seeing community (and therefore seeing myself) is restored, my sense of bliss wakes up more with every pelt of paint and smear of oil. It is enough to acknowledge that the Jouvay procession moves along Empire Boulevard.

The Jouvay itself is always about interrupting Empire; and the police, and colonization, and gentrification, and grief, and poverty, and institutions, and white supremacy. In 2020 we are locating ‘home’ in new and different ways. What will the Empire look like in the next decade? What kind of procession are we making?

\- Arielle M. John -
.

Since the first time I experienced Jouvay in Brooklyn, it felt like a common ground... waves of Carib beings, different but together, waving to organized rhythms and sounds, each island with their own colors and still collectively powerful, emancipating and healing themselves in accordance to one spirit : Carnival.

Nonm jòdi sé yè a nonm dèmen (Today’s man is the past version of the one tomorrow) is a motto I use to define myself and my creative process. It questions time, existence and becoming! The Brooklyn Jouvay short film is tied to the pandemic, it is a statement. It is a creative and collective process of art-making. It's educational and part of Caribbeing’s mission. When I watched Arielle John’s reaction the first time she looked at the raw edit that led her to immediately share her poem with me, the spirit of Carnival had already tied everything together. Instinctively, Arielle’s poem fit into the intention of the film.

Brooklyn’s Jouvay is part of a whole Caribbean Collective. It’s a unique form that belongs to those distant relatives. There is no end, no limit to the Culture. Past, Present and Future are combined in our bodies. The opening of the new day lay in our comprehension of ourselves.

\- slasieye -

I AM CARIBBEING is an award-winning cultural venture illuminating the Caribbean experience and communities in Greater New York City and around the world. We work at the intersection of Culture x Community x Commerce. Based in Flatbush, Brooklyn, AKA the “Little Caribbean,” I AM Caribbeing spans contemporary culture and is a hub for creativity and collaborations with some of the Caribbean and its diaspora’s most visionary talent and innovative brands. Now in its twelfth year, I AM CARIBBEING has grown into a global movement.
